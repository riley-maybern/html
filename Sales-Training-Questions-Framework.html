Two Things Drive Every Deal Forward

Every meeting in the arc has two engines. Understand these and you'll never wonder "what was the point of that call?"
↻
Belief Shift
The change in what the buyer believes after a meeting that they didn't believe before it.

Example:
Before the Value Creation workshop: "We're probably fine."
After: "The cost of doing nothing is real and material."
✓
Proof of Shift
The observable action the buyer takes that proves the belief actually landed — not just a head-nod.

Example:
They don't just say "makes sense" —
They lock a Process Review date and articulate ROI internally to their boss.
Why this matters: If you can't name the belief shift for a meeting, you don't know why you're having it. If you can't spot the proof, you don't know if it worked.

THE TOOLKIT

Two Types of Questions

Every question you ask falls into one of two categories. Knowing which you're using — and when — is the whole game.

Advancing Questions

Move the deal forward. Open new territory. Create urgency by uncovering what the buyer hasn't considered.

Confirming Questions

Lock in progress. Verify understanding. Ensure the buyer has internalized the last belief shift before moving on.

Advancing Questions

These open new territory and create momentum. They're the engine of deal progression.
01

Uncover Hidden Pain

"What happens downstream when that process breaks?"

02

Challenge Assumptions

"What if the real bottleneck isn't where you think it is?"

03

Create Vision

"If this worked perfectly, what would your team's day look like?"

CONFIRMING QUESTIONS

Lock In the Shift

Without confirming questions, buyers nod along but never internalize. These make belief shifts stick.

Verify Understanding

"Just to make sure I have this right — the main issue is X impacting Y?"

Test Commitment

"If we could solve that, would it be worth bringing your VP into the conversation?"

The movie test: In great films, every scene changes something. If you remove it and nothing changes, it shouldn't be there. Apply this to meetings — every meeting should shift a belief.
INITIAL CALL

Initial Call Questions

Belief shift: "I have a structural risk I haven't fully examined."
Advancing
"Walk me through who actually owns the waterfall logic today — and what happens when that person is unavailable for a quarter-end."
Advancing
"When an LP asks you to defend a specific calculation, what does the process look like to trace it back to the source?"
Advancing
"What would happen to your next distribution cycle if your lead fund accountant left tomorrow?"
Confirming
"Based on what we've discussed, it sounds like there are a few structural risks worth examining more closely — would you agree?"
INITIAL CALL — CONTINUED

Going Deeper on the Initial Call

The best first calls feel like a conversation, not an interrogation.
Advancing
"When you think about scaling from your current fund count to double that, where does the process start to feel fragile?"
Advancing
"If you fast-forward six quarters, what part of your current process worries you most?"
Confirming
"If we could show you how firms with similar complexity have addressed these exact gaps, would it be worth 45 minutes with your CFO / Head of Fund Ops?"
Balance: The first call is discovery-led, but it's also where you lightly position Maybern as creating its own category — the performance book of record. Plant the seed, don't sell the tree. Your job is to learn enough to earn the second meeting while leaving them curious about a category they didn't know existed.
TECHNIQUE

The Pain Ladder

Three levels of depth. Most reps stay at Level 1. The best reps live at Level 3.
1
Surface

"What challenges are you facing?"

2
Impact

"What happens to the team when that breaks?"

3
Personal

"How does that affect your ability to hit your goals?"

PAIN LADDER IN ACTION

Pain Ladder Applied

Here's the same technique in a real Maybern conversation flow.
Level 1 — Surface
"What's your biggest challenge with fund administration right now?"
Level 2 — Impact
"When capital calls are delayed, how does that ripple through to your LPs?"
Level 3 — Personal
"What does that mean for your relationship with those LPs at year-end review?"
Why it works: Level 3 questions connect the business problem to personal stakes. That's where urgency lives.

Going to Level 3

The trick isn't asking harder questions. It's earning permission to go deeper.

How to Earn Depth

Reflect back what they said at Level 1 before asking Level 2. Acknowledge the impact before going personal.

Watch for Signals

When a buyer pauses, sighs, or says "honestly..." — you've hit something real. Slow down and explore.

If your discovery sounds like a tax audit, you haven't earned Level 3.
VALUE CREATION WORKSHOP

Value Creation Workshop Questions

Belief shift: "The cost of inaction is quantifiable and material — this isn't just an efficiency play."
Advancing
"How many person-hours per quarter does your team spend on waterfall calculations, validation, and LP-facing reconciliation?"
Advancing
"If you translated those hours into fully-loaded annual cost, what does that number look like?"
Advancing
"What happens to that number if your fund count doubles in the next 18 months — does it scale linearly or worse?"
Confirming
"Now that we've quantified the cost side — does this feel like a material enough problem to warrant a serious evaluation?"
MID-CYCLE QUESTIONS

Process Review & Tailored Demo

Two pivotal meetings: mapping fragilities (M5) and proving it works with real data (M8).

Process Review

Advancing
"Let's map your process end-to-end: starting from when fund terms are set, walk me through every step until an LP receives their distribution statement."
Confirming
"Now that we've mapped this out, do the fragility points we identified feel accurate — or are there others?"

Tailored Demo

Advancing
"You're looking at your actual fund structures in the system — does the output match what you'd expect to see?"
Confirming
"Based on what you've seen with your own data, does this feel like a real solution for your team — or are there gaps?"
WARNING SIGNS

Mind the Gaps

These are the top deal-stalling patterns we see. Each is a missed or incomplete belief shift.

Champion Gap

You're selling to someone who can't buy. Find the economic buyer early.

Pain Gap

Buyer acknowledges the problem but hasn't quantified the cost. No urgency.

Consensus Gap

Your champion is sold, but they can't convince their peers. Need multi-threading.

More Deal Gaps

Two more patterns that stall deals in the late stages.

Proof Gap

Buyer likes the pitch but hasn't seen evidence it works for their use case. Offer a reference or pilot.

Timing Gap

Everything's aligned but there's no trigger event. Help them build a business case with a deadline.

CLOSING THE GAPS

Gap Closing Questions

When you spot a gap, these questions from the framework can help close it.

Champion Gap

Advancing
"Who has the authority to sign off on a new system or process change in fund operations — and what does their approval process look like?"
Confirming
"What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable recommending that next step internally?"

Pain Gap

Advancing
"How many person-hours per quarter does your team spend on waterfall calculations, validation, and LP-facing reconciliation?"
Confirming
"Now that we've quantified the cost side — does this feel like a material enough problem to warrant a serious evaluation?"
CLOSING THE GAPS — CONTINUED

More Gap Closers

Consensus Gap

Advancing
"If you were to evaluate a platform like this properly, who would need to be involved to make sure nothing gets missed?"
Confirming
"It sounds like we have the right stakeholders identified — does that feel complete, or is anyone missing?"

Proof Gap

Advancing
"Looking at the files you've shared, how well do these represent the full range of waterfall tier structures across your funds?"
Confirming
"Based on what we've reviewed, do you feel confident the dataset captures enough complexity for a realistic demo?"

Timing Gap

Advancing
"Is there an event on your calendar — a fundraise, an audit, a fund launch — that creates natural urgency around resolving this?"
Confirming
"If we mapped out a timeline with clear milestones and owners, would your team be able to commit to that cadence?"

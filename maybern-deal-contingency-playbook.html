01

System Overview

The principle, the model, and how to use this document.

The Principle

Eliminate Hesitation

You’re on a call. Your champion just told you she’s leaving the firm. Your stomach drops. You have about four seconds before you need to say something — and whatever comes out of your mouth next will determine whether this deal survives or dies quietly over the next three weeks.

That moment — the gap between the surprise and the response — is where deals go to die. Not because reps are bad at their jobs, but because improvisation under pressure is unreliable. Some days you nail it. Some days you freeze. The purpose of this playbook is to close that gap entirely.

The Walsh Principle

“If I had to write a job description for a head coach, I would say the most important quality is the ability to calmly and intelligently respond when things go wrong.” — Bill Walsh, The Score Takes Care of Itself

Walsh didn’t wait for the game to tell him what to do. He scripted his first 25 plays before kickoff. His coaching staff had the full playbook. His quarterback had a wristband with 20 plays. His lineman knew his assignment and nothing else. Everyone had exactly the depth they needed — no more, no less. The score took care of itself because the preparation was already done.

This document applies the same architecture to our sales team. Every scenario in here has four components: early warning signals (so you see it coming), an immediate response protocol (so you know your first three moves), a recovery play (so you have a plan for weeks 1–4), and kill criteria (so you know when to walk away instead of pouring good effort after bad).

Signal Sheet

AE quick reference. Signals and first moves for all eleven scenarios.

AE Quick Reference

Deal Contingency Signal Sheet

When you spot a signal, brief your manager within 4 hours, diagnose together, then execute the first move.

01

Champion Departure

Signals
  • Response times lengthen from hours to days
  • Champion delegates meeting attendance
  • LinkedIn updated or “Open to Work”
  • “I may not be the right person to drive this”
First Move
  • Reach champion personally — congratulate, don’t panic-sell
  • Three asks: Who now? Intro? Where are you headed?
  • Contact every other person at the account
  • If $300K+: escalate for exec outreach in 48 hrs
02

“Do Nothing” Reasserts

Signals
  • Questions dry up — stops asking about timelines
  • Starts defending spreadsheets or their controller
  • Timeline shifts to “next year”
  • “What we have works okay for now”
First Move
  • DON’T sell harder. Diagnose the root cause.
  • Reflect their pain: “You told me [X]. Has that changed?”
  • If no ROI: request cost-of-status-quo session
  • If controller fear: get controller hands-on with their data
03

Budget Reprioritization

Signals
  • “Budget is getting tighter” or “leadership pulling back”
  • Approval gains new steps or stakeholders
  • EB stops attending; scope reduction requests
First Move
  • Ask: “Budget problem or priority problem?”
  • If budget: reframe as cost savings / ROI payback
  • If priority: identify what displaced you
  • Offer phased commercial options
04

Legal / Procurement Stall

Signals
  • Verbal commit weeks ago, no redlines
  • Security questionnaire late in cycle
  • Champion quiet after “it’s just legal now”
First Move
  • Get direct legal/procurement access
  • “Normal process or actual concern?”
  • Pre-send full security package
  • Set mutual close plan with dates
05

Competitive Ambush

Signals
  • Suddenly asks for feature comparisons
  • “Also looking at [competitor]” surfaces late
  • Incumbent offers surprise discount
  • “Best and final” out of sequence
First Move
  • Don’t panic-discount. “What changed?”
  • Reanchor on original decision criteria
  • If incumbent: “Why didn’t they offer this before?”
  • Hold price, add value. Deploy reference.
06

“We’ll Build It Ourselves”

Signals
  • Asks detailed technical/architecture questions
  • “Our data engineer thinks he could build this”
  • Internal prototype or AI-built calculator mentioned
  • “With AI tools now, how hard could this be?”
First Move
  • Don’t dismiss. “You’re right about v1. What about v2–v200?”
  • Surface the iceberg: audit, edge cases, maintenance, key-person risk
  • Diagnose type: real builder, vibes, or cost objection?
  • Offer technical deep dive, not more selling.
07

Ownership Stall

Signals
  • Expanding stakeholder list, new names each meeting
  • “We need to align internally”
  • Same meeting held twice with different audiences
  • Alignment meeting keeps getting cancelled
First Move
  • Ask: “Who signs the contract?”
  • Propose joint session with RACI chart
  • If committee: coach champion on internal pitch
  • Get everyone in one room. Stop parallel tracks.
08

Capacity Crunch

Signals
  • Meetings rescheduled repeatedly
  • “After audit season / fund close / year-end”
  • Champion goes dark — overwhelm, not avoidance
  • “Timing isn’t right” without specific date
First Move
  • “If bandwidth weren’t an issue, would you move forward?”
  • Offer zero-burden start while they’re busy
  • Get specific re-engagement date on calendar
  • Present phased option: one fund, limited scope.
09

Scar Tissue

Signals
  • Unprompted mention of prior vendor failure
  • Excessive due diligence, 5+ reference requests
  • “We tried this before and it was a disaster”
  • Emotional language: “nightmare,” “nearly got fired”
First Move
  • Listen first. Don’t say “we’re different.”
  • “What specifically went wrong?” — diagnose before prescribing
  • Offer pilot with real data, not more demos
  • Deploy matched reference: client who was also burned.
10

M&A Freeze

Signals
  • Champion goes abruptly dark, vague language
  • “Can’t really get into it” — careful, lawyered tone
  • All vendor decisions frozen “until further notice”
  • Industry rumors, LinkedIn changes, hiring freeze
First Move
  • Don’t push for details they can’t share
  • Protect the relationship. Be patient, not persistent.
  • Research the acquirer/new leadership
  • Set 60–90 day re-engagement reminder.
11

Power Line Block

Signals
  • “I’ll run it up the flagpole” repeatedly
  • Access to EB deflected: “They trust my recommendation”
  • Proposal in “review” 3+ weeks, no feedback
  • Contact can’t describe decision path with names
First Move
  • Diagnose: blocking, unable, or unaware?
  • Deploy Maybern exec engagement: CEO-to-CFO peer meeting
  • Arm champion with exec summary and ROI model
  • Create a reason for access, not a demand.

Pipeline Review Questions — Ask Every Week

  • If your champion left tomorrow, who else takes your call?
  • Can the champion state cost of status quo in dollars without your help?
  • What’s more likely to beat us — a competitor or “do nothing”?
  • Is there a compelling event or just internal motivation?
  • Has the controller seen a hands-on demo with their data?
  • What happens if they do nothing for 12 months?
  • Is legal/procurement engaged or are we assuming that’s easy?
  • When was the EB last directly engaged?

Champion Departure Mid-Cycle

Your internal advocate leaves, gets promoted, or goes silent.

Context

Why This Is Your #1 Deal Risk

You’ve spent four months building this deal. Discovery went deep. The demo was flawless. Your champion told you last week she was “taking this to the partners next Tuesday.” Then Monday morning, you see the LinkedIn post: “Excited to announce I’m joining [Other Firm] as Head of Fund Operations.”

In enterprise sales, losing a champion mid-cycle is the equivalent of losing your starting quarterback in the third quarter. The game doesn’t stop — but everything you’ve built suddenly runs through someone who doesn’t know the playbook, doesn’t feel the urgency, and didn’t choose to be in this game.

02

“Do Nothing” Reasserts Late

Your most dangerous competitor isn’t a product. It’s inertia.

Context

Why Status Quo Is Our Real Competitor

The call was going well. Then the CFO leaned back and said: “Look, I appreciate everything you’ve shown us. But honestly? Sarah has things running pretty smoothly. We might revisit this after our next fund close.”

That sentence — polite, reasonable, delivered with a smile — is the sound of a deal dying. Not with a dramatic loss to a competitor, but with a slow fade back into the comfort of the familiar. It’s death by spreadsheet.

The Uncomfortable Truth

“The most dangerous competitor isn’t the one who builds a better product. It’s the one who convinces your prospect that their current pain is manageable.”

PE fund accounting has operated on spreadsheets, legacy platforms, and manual processes for decades. Prospects aren’t comparing us to Allvue — they’re comparing us to the devil they know. And the devil they know has a name, a desk down the hall, and 15 years of institutional knowledge that no software demo can replicate in 60 minutes.

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Engagement Cooling

Meetings shortened. Questions shift to “timing.” Internal stakeholders stop showing. Discovery answers go missing.

Warning

Competing Priorities

Fund raise, audit, leadership change. “Still important, but we have things to get through.” Timeline to “next year.”

Critical

Rationalization Language

“What we have works okay.” “We hired someone to handle this.” “Team is worried about disruption.” Any sentence reframing pain as manageable.

The Deadly Tells

Two signals that the deal is slipping back to status quo: (1) Questions dry up. A prospect who’s still imagining a future with your product asks about integrations, timelines, edge cases. When the questions stop, they’ve stopped imagining. (2) They start defending their spreadsheets. “Our process is actually pretty solid” is not an observation — it’s a rationalization. Nobody describes something that’s truly working well as “pretty solid.”

Diagnosis

Four Root Causes

Most Common

A: Pain Wasn’t Quantified

They acknowledged the problem but you never gave it a number. Without dollars, the pain stays abstract.

  • Test: Can the champion state cost of status quo in dollars without your help?
High Frequency

B: No Compelling Event

No deadline forcing a decision. Chronic pain, not acute. Always a reason to wait.

  • Test: What happens if they do nothing for 12 months?
High Impact

C: Fear of Leaving Excel and the Controller

Not a tech objection — a people and identity objection. The firm trusts the person who built the spreadsheets more than any software. Moving to Maybern feels like devaluing their most trusted operator.

  • Test: “Who would be most affected, and how do they feel?”
  • Deeper fear: “What if the system can’t handle what [person] does?”
Structural

D: Champion Can’t Sell Internally

Convinced but can’t get the EB to prioritize. Lacks org capital, storytelling, or access.

  • Test: Have they presented to the EB, or “waiting for the right time”?
Recovery

  • Audit: “How confident in manual calculations?”

C: Excel and the Controller

The Reframe

Maybern doesn’t replace your best people. It frees them. The Controller spending 60% of their time on spreadsheets isn’t doing their highest-value work — they’re doing data entry with a finance degree. That’s like hiring a chef and asking them to wash dishes all day.

01

Convert Controller to Champion

Hands-on session with their data. Show it handles edge cases. Career upgrade, not replacement.

02

Reframe the Role

“Spreadsheet maintainer becomes fund ops strategist.”

03

Surface Key-Person Risk

“What happens if [controller] is out during quarter-end?” Single point of failure.

D: Champion Can’t Sell Internally

Arm the Champion

  • Dry-run internal presentation. Prep for objections.
  • “All you need is 20 min with [EB].”

  • Ask permission first. If champion resists, that’s a signal.
Option 3

Executive Alignment

  • Ross reaches out peer-to-peer as industry conversation
  • Get EB thinking about the problem before champion makes the ask
Scripts

Exact Language — “Do Nothing”

“What we have works okay for now.”

“When you say ‘works okay,’ does that include the [pain from discovery]? You mentioned it was costing [X]. Has that gotten better, or has your team gotten used to working around it?”

If they mention the controller: “I don’t doubt [controller] is great. If they were out two weeks during quarter-end, what happens?”

“Let’s revisit next quarter.”

No answer = dead deal. An answer = your roadmap.

“We hired someone to handle this.”

“That’s great. Firms that get the most from Maybern pair a strong ops hire with modern tooling. It lets that person focus on strategy instead of rebuilding spreadsheets. Worth looping them in?”

When the Controller Is in the Room

“[Controller], you know this business inside and out. No system replaces that. What we hear from fund accountants who switched is Maybern lets them stop spending time on mechanical work. I’d love to show you how [specific scenario] works and get your honest reaction.”

Talking to CFO About the Controller

“Is [controller] doing their highest-value work? They’re spending [X hrs/week] on calculations. That’s data entry with a finance title. What would it mean if they had that time back?”

Prevention

Making “Do Nothing” Harder to Choose

01

Quantify Pain in Discovery

Never leave discovery without a dollar figure. “How many hours per week does your team spend on manual waterfall calculations?” Translate every pain point into FTE hours, loaded cost, or risk exposure. Abstract pain loses to concrete inertia.

02

Deliver ROI Before They Ask

Don’t wait for a “do nothing” objection to build the business case. Proactively deliver a cost-of-status-quo analysis after discovery. When the CFO asks “why now?” your champion already has the answer.

03

Find the Compelling Event Early

In MEDDPICC, the “I” (Identify Pain) and “D” (Decision Criteria) should surface a forcing function. Fund launch, audit, LP demands, key person risk. If there’s no event, you’re relying on motivation alone — and motivation fades.

04

Get the Controller Involved Early

If the Controller or fund accountant is going to be a blocker, find out in discovery, not at proposal. Get them hands-on with their data during the evaluation so they’re a champion by the time the decision comes, not a surprise objection.

05

Create Future-State Visualization

During demos, don’t just show features. Show their life after Maybern: “Instead of spending 3 days on quarter-end waterfall calcs, your team runs it in 20 minutes and spends the rest on LP reporting.” Make the gap between today and tomorrow vivid.

06

Pipeline Review: “What Beats Us?”

Every pipeline review, ask: “What’s more likely to beat us here — a competitor or do nothing?” If the answer is do nothing and the AE hasn’t quantified the pain or found a compelling event, that’s this week’s action item.

03

Economic Buyer Reprioritizes Budget

The money was there. Now it’s not — or it’s going somewhere else.

Context

How Budget Shifts Hit PE Firms

The email arrives at 7:14 AM: “Hey, wanted to give you a heads up — our Managing Partner has asked everyone to pull back on new spending for the next two quarters. This isn’t about Maybern, it’s just timing.”

“It’s not about you” is the five most dangerous words in enterprise sales, because they’re usually true — and that makes them feel like there’s nothing you can do. But “nothing you can do” is almost never accurate. It just means the frame has changed, and you need to change your frame faster than the prospect changes their mind.

PE firms don’t operate like corporations with annual IT budgets. Spending decisions are made by a small group of partners, often informally, and can change overnight. This means a budget shift is both more sudden and more absolute than at a corporate — but it also means the reverse is true. If you reframe Maybern as aligned with their new priority, you can get re-approved just as quickly as you got deprioritized.

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Pace Changes

Approval steps multiply. New stakeholders introduced late. Champion asks about phased pricing unprompted.

Warning

Organizational Stress

Fund underperformance, delayed raise, portfolio issues. Hiring freezes. “Things are tight right now.”

Critical

Explicit Budget Language

“We need to pause on new spending.” EB cancels meetings. Champion says “I still want to, but…”

Diagnosis

Budget Problem vs. Priority Problem

The single most important diagnostic. The response is completely different.

Budget Freeze (Money Gone)

  • Firm-wide pause — not about you
  • Triggered by macro: fund performance, markets, LP pressure
  • Usually has a timeline
  • Play: Reframe as cost savings. Show ROI payback <12 months. “Is something that saves money exempt?”

Priority Shift (Money Redirected)

  • Something else won the internal competition
  • Portfolio emergency, new fund, compliance issue
  • Often temporary but feels permanent
  • Play: Identify what displaced you, when it resolves, align with new priority if possible.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response

Hour 0–4: Validate

Don’t Accept the Frame

  • “Is this a budget constraint or a priority shift? Our response would be different.”
  • If freeze: “Could a project demonstrating cost savings still get approved?”
  • If priority: “What displaced this, and when does it resolve?”
Hour 4–24: Reframe

Position as Part of the Solution

  • If freeze: rapid ROI showing cost savings and payback. Efficiency investment, not new expense.
  • If priority shift: align with new priority. Fund launch? Maybern accelerates ops. Compliance? Reduces audit risk.
  • If neither: propose phased commercial model.
Hour 24–48: Secure Terms

No Open-Ended Pause

  • Negotiate re-engagement: specific date, condition, person.
  • “Can we agree on [date] to reconnect?”
  • No date or condition = dead deal, not paused. Nurture.
Recovery

01

Fund launch displaced you? Maybern is critical infrastructure for new fund. Compliance issue? Audit risk reduction. Become part of what they’re spending on.

02

Full migration off table, but targeted solution isn’t. “Just solve waterfall calcs for new fund. 4-week implementation, immediate impact.”

03

Specific re-engagement trigger: “When fund raise closes.” “After audit season.” Calendar it. Follow up exactly when promised.

Scripts

Legal / Procurement Stall

The deal was done. Then it entered the black hole.

Context

Why Legal Stalls Are Uniquely Dangerous

Three weeks ago, the CFO shook your hand and said “let’s do this.” You updated the CRM, told your manager it was closing this month, maybe even mentally spent the commission. Now you’re staring at an inbox with two unanswered follow-ups and a champion who says “I think they’re still reviewing it.”

The Trap

“It’s just legal now” is the most dangerous sentence in B2B sales. It tells the AE to stop selling. But legal review is where deals go to be quietly reconsidered, deprioritized, or killed by people who weren’t in the room when the decision was made.

Competitive Ambush

An incumbent discounts or a new vendor enters late to create leverage.

Context

How Competition Works in PE Fund Accounting

You’re at proposal stage. The deal is tracking. Then on today’s call, the champion drops it casually: “By the way, our current vendor reached out with a pretty aggressive offer. Can you sharpen your pencil?”

Your pulse spikes. The instinct is immediate: match the price, protect the deal, do whatever it takes. That instinct is wrong. It’s the sales equivalent of a goalkeeper diving before the penalty is kicked — you’re reacting to where you think the ball is going instead of reading the play.

The Insight That Changes Everything

In most competitive situations in our market, the competitor isn’t actually a threat to win. They’re a tool the prospect is using to improve their deal with you. Recognizing the difference between a genuine evaluation and a leverage play is the single most valuable skill in this scenario.

True competitive evaluations are relatively rare in PE fund accounting. Most of our deals are won or lost against the status quo. But when competition appears, it usually takes one of two forms: the incumbent offering a last-minute discount to retain the account, or the prospect introducing a second vendor late in the process to create pricing leverage. Both feel urgent. Neither requires panic.

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Information Requests Change

Detailed feature comparison matrices requested. New eval criteria appear. “Walk us through your approach to [very specific capability]” without context.

Warning

Competitor Surfaces

“We’re also talking to [vendor].” Incumbent reaches out with new pricing. Prospect mentions capabilities aligned with competitor’s strengths.

Critical

Leverage Indicators

“Best and final pricing” out of sequence. Won’t share competing offer details. Timeline suddenly accelerates. Champion becomes evasive.

Diagnosis

Genuine Evaluation vs. Leverage Play

Genuine Competitive Eval

  • Transparent about who else they’re evaluating
  • Detailed questions on differentiation
  • Both vendors given equal access and timelines
  • Criteria established before competitor entered
  • Play: Win on product and value. Never price alone.

Leverage Play

  • Competitor appears suddenly, late
  • Won’t share specifics of competing offer
  • Conversation jumps to pricing, not capabilities
  • “Best and final” without context
  • Play: Hold price. Add value. Call it out gently.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response

Hour 0–4: Diagnose

Don’t Panic-Discount

  • Every dollar cut now sets the precedent for the entire relationship.
  • “Help me understand what’s changed. What are you seeing from them?”
  • Listen: specific capabilities (genuine) vs. just pricing (leverage).
Hour 4–12: Reanchor

Pull Back to Value

  • “Before pricing — let’s go back to original requirements. Which does the other option address?”
  • Don’t let new criteria favoring competitor redefine the evaluation.
  • For incumbents: “If they could solve this at this price, why didn’t they before you looked elsewhere?”
Hour 12–48: Strengthen

Add Value, Don’t Cut Price

  • Deploy reference who evaluated same competitor and chose Maybern.
  • If adjusting commercially: add value (extended onboarding, faster implementation, better terms) vs. reduce price.
  • If leverage play, name it gently: “If there are things they do better, let’s address those. If this is about price, let’s have that conversation directly.”

“We’ll Build It Ourselves”

AI made building easy. It didn’t make building the right choice.

Context

The New Objection That Didn’t Exist Two Years Ago

You’re mid-demo. The COO’s been quiet for ten minutes, watching the waterfall calculation engine work. Then he leans forward and says: “This is impressive. But honestly — with AI tools now, couldn’t we just build something like this ourselves?”

Two years ago, that question would have been absurd. Fund accounting software is complex, regulated, and took Maybern years and millions to build. But we don’t live in that world anymore. The COO’s nephew built a functioning app over a weekend with Cursor. A partner at the firm saw a LinkedIn post about “vibe coding.” An analyst spun up a waterfall calculator in Claude in 30 minutes. The question isn’t crazy — it’s just incomplete.

The Iceberg

Building v1 of anything is now trivially easy. Building v1 of fund accounting software that handles 47 different waterfall structures, survives an audit, scales across 12 funds, and doesn’t break when your fund accountant quits — that’s a different conversation entirely. Your job isn’t to argue that AI can’t build things. It’s to make them see the 90% of the iceberg that’s underwater.

This scenario is structurally different from the other scenarios. It’s not something that happens to your deal — it’s a belief system the prospect walks in with, or develops mid-cycle when they see a demo and think “that doesn’t look that hard.” It’s a cousin of “Do Nothing” (it’s a form of status quo preference dressed up as innovation), but the recovery is completely different because you’re not fighting inertia. You’re fighting overconfidence.

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Curiosity Phase

Prospect asks unusually detailed technical questions about your architecture. “What’s your tech stack?” “How do you handle the calculation logic?” They’re mentally reverse-engineering, not evaluating.

Warning

Internal Momentum

Champion mentions an internal team member “playing around with something.” “Our data engineer thinks he could prototype this.” An analyst has already built a waterfall calculator in a spreadsheet or script. Someone at the firm has been talking about AI capabilities.

Critical

Active Build Consideration

“We’re thinking about building this in-house.” “With AI tools now, how hard could this be?” Timeline suddenly pauses while they “explore alternatives.” A technology leader or partner who wasn’t in the evaluation starts driving the conversation.

The Tell

The prospect stops asking about your product’s capabilities and starts asking about how it works technically. They’re not buying — they’re scoping a build. When questions shift from “can it do X?” to “how does it do X?” without a technical evaluation reason, they’re mentally building a spec.

Diagnosis

Three Versions of “Build It Ourselves”

Most Dangerous

Type 1: The Confident Builder

Someone at the firm — a data engineer, a tech-savvy partner, or an ops person who’s been coding with AI — genuinely believes they can build a replacement. They may have already built a prototype. They have internal credibility and momentum.

  • Why it’s dangerous: They might actually build something that works for 3 months. The failure mode is delayed, not immediate — and by the time it fails, your deal window has closed.
High Frequency

Type 2: The Vibes-Based Objection

Nobody at the firm is actually going to build anything. But a partner read an article about AI, saw a demo of Cursor, or heard at a conference that “you can build anything with AI now.” The objection is based on a vague sense that buying software is now optional.

  • Why it’s common: AI hype is everywhere. Non-technical executives massively overestimate what “build it with AI” means in practice. This is the most addressable version.
High Impact

Type 3: The Cost Rationalization

“Build it ourselves” is cover for a different objection — usually price. They don’t actually want to build. They want to use the build option as leverage to negotiate a lower price, or to justify delaying the purchase to their partners.

  • Diagnostic: Ask: “If we could solve the cost concern, would you still want to build?” If they say no, this is a pricing conversation, not a build conversation.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response Protocol

Hour 0–4: Don’t Dismiss, Don’t Panic

Validate and Diagnose

  • The worst response is condescension: “Fund accounting is too complex to build.” They’ll hear arrogance and dig in.
  • The second worst is panic: immediately dropping price or adding features.
  • The right move: Take it seriously and ask diagnostic questions. “That’s a fair question. Help me understand — what specifically are you thinking about building? And who on your team would own it?”
  • Listen for which type this is: Confident Builder, Vibes-Based, or Cost Rationalization.
Hour 4–12: Surface the Iceberg

Make the Invisible Visible

  • Don’t argue about whether AI can build v1. Concede it: “You’re right — you could probably build a basic waterfall calculator in a few weeks.”
  • Then pivot: “The question isn’t whether you can build v1. It’s what happens at v2, v5, and v20 — when you add a new fund structure, when audit season hits, when the person who built it leaves, when LP reporting requirements change.”
  • Frame the total cost: build cost + maintenance cost + opportunity cost of senior people maintaining it + risk cost of errors.
Hour 12–48: Reframe the Decision

Build vs. Buy Is the Wrong Frame

  • The real question isn’t “can we build it?” It’s “should our fund accountants be maintaining software, or should they be running fund operations?”
  • For PE firms: “Your competitive advantage is deal sourcing, portfolio management, and LP relationships. It is not maintaining a bespoke waterfall calculation engine.”
  • Deploy a reference: a client who considered building and chose Maybern, or better yet, a client who tried building first and then came to Maybern.
The Argument

The Build vs. Buy Iceberg

The prospect sees the tip. Your job is to walk them around to the other side.

What They See (Above the Waterline)

  • AI can generate working code in minutes
  • A waterfall calculator prototype works after a weekend
  • No annual software cost
  • “We control it and can customize anything”
  • Internal team already has skills

What They Don’t See (Below the Waterline)

  • 47+ waterfall structure variations across fund types
  • Audit trail requirements — every calculation traceable
  • LP reporting in multiple formats and frequencies
  • Handling edge cases: clawbacks, catch-ups, preferred returns
  • Version control across multiple fund vintages
  • Security, access controls, SOC 2 compliance
  • Integrating with fund admin, banking, portfolio systems
  • Maintaining it when the builder leaves
  • Updating it when regulations change
  • Supporting it at 2 AM during quarter-end close
The Analogy That Lands

Building fund accounting software with AI is like building a house with a nail gun. The nail gun is incredible — it’s 10x faster than a hammer. But the nail gun doesn’t know where the studs go, it doesn’t know building code, and it definitely doesn’t know that the foundation needs to support a second story you haven’t planned yet. AI is the best tool ever invented for building things fast. It has no opinion on whether you should be building them at all.

Internal Ownership Stall

Two groups. Two agendas. Zero decision.

Context

The Deal That Dies in the Hallway

Everything looked right. Champion was bought in. Demo went well. Budget existed. Then you hear: “We need to align internally before we can move forward.” That was six weeks ago.

At PE firms, fund accounting lives at the exact seam between Finance and Technology. The CFO or Controller owns the process. The CTO or Head of IT owns the infrastructure. Neither wants to own a software decision that crosses into the other’s territory. Add a partnership structure where any senior partner can raise a hand and slow things down, and you have the perfect recipe for a deal that everyone likes and nobody signs.

The Dynamic

This isn’t indecision. It’s structural. The firm doesn’t have a clear owner for technology decisions that affect finance workflows. Your deal fell into the gap between two org chart boxes, and nobody is incentivized to pull it out.

This scenario is different from Legal Stall (where one group is blocking) and different from Do Nothing (where there’s no urgency). Here, multiple people want to move forward — they just can’t agree on who leads, who pays, who implements, or whose team absorbs the change.

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Expanding Stakeholder List

New names keep appearing. “We need to loop in IT.” “Partners want to weigh in.” Each meeting adds someone but never consolidates to a decision-maker.

Warning

Circular Conversations

You’ve had the same meeting twice with different audiences. Questions you answered for Finance get re-asked by Technology. Nobody references what the other group said.

Critical

The Alignment Meeting That Never Happens

“We just need to get everyone in a room.” That room has been booked and cancelled three times. Or the meeting happened, but no decision came out of it. “Good discussion, need to follow up.”

The Tell

Ask: “Who signs the contract?” If the answer involves more than one name, a committee, or “we need to figure that out” — you are in an ownership stall. The deal has no single throat to choke.

Diagnosis

High Impact

High Frequency

  • Diagnostic: “Is there anyone on the team who has concerns about this change?” If the champion hedges, there’s an internal adversary.

Capacity Crunch

They want it. They can’t absorb it. Not now.

Context

The Calendar Is the Competitor

The champion calls you on a Tuesday. You can hear the exhaustion. “Look, I love what you showed us. But we just launched Fund VII, audit starts in three weeks, and my team is drowning. Can we pick this up after things calm down?”

This isn’t a no. It’s not budget. It’s not politics. It’s physics. The firm’s operational team is running at 110% capacity, and absorbing a new software implementation — even one that ultimately saves time — requires bandwidth they literally do not have right now.

The Paradox

The firms that need Maybern most urgently are the ones least able to implement it. Their pain is real — they’re drowning in manual processes, audit prep, and fund launches. But the same pain that makes them a perfect prospect also makes them unable to absorb change. You’re selling a lifeboat to people who are too busy bailing water to climb aboard.

The danger isn’t that they say no. It’s that they say “later” — and later never comes. Audit season ends, and then it’s fundraising. Fundraising ends, and then it’s year-end close. The calendar is always full. “After things calm down” is the PE firm equivalent of “when I have time to go to the gym.”

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Scheduling Difficulty

Meetings get rescheduled. Response times stretch. “Sorry, things are crazy right now.” Not avoidance — they’re genuinely overwhelmed.

Warning

Timing Deflection

“We’ll look at this after audit season.” “Let’s revisit after the fund launch.” “Can we push this to Q3?” The language is always future-tense and tied to an operational milestone.

Critical

Indefinite Pause

“We love this but the timing just isn’t right.” No specific date for re-engagement. Champion goes dark. Not because they lost interest — because they got buried.

The Tell

Ask: “If your team had unlimited bandwidth, would you move forward?” If the answer is yes without hesitation, this is a capacity problem, not a conviction problem. That distinction changes everything about your approach.

Diagnosis

Three Versions of the Capacity Crunch

Most Common

Type A: Cyclical Overload

Audit season. Year-end close. Fund launch. LP annual meeting prep. These are predictable capacity crunches that happen at the same time every year. The deal stalls not because of a surprise but because of a calendar everyone knew about.

  • Key insight: If you didn’t plan your sales cycle around their operational calendar, the stall is partially your fault. This is preventable.
High Impact

Type B: Structural Understaffing

The team is always at capacity. Not because of a seasonal crunch, but because they’re permanently understaffed for the work they do. There is no “after things calm down” — things never calm down.

  • Key insight: This is actually your strongest selling point. “If your team is always at 110%, the question isn’t whether you can afford to implement Maybern. It’s whether you can afford not to.”
Medium

Type C: Capacity as Polite Deflection

“We don’t have bandwidth” is sometimes the professional way of saying “this isn’t a priority.” The team is busy, but so is every team. The real issue is that Maybern hasn’t risen high enough on the priority list to justify the disruption.

  • Diagnostic: “If this would save your team 15 hours a week within 90 days, would you find the bandwidth to implement?” If they hesitate, this is a priority problem, not a capacity problem.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response Protocol

Hour 0–4: Validate and Diagnose

Is This Real or Polite?

  • Ask the diagnostic question: “If bandwidth weren’t a constraint, would you move forward?”
  • If yes: this is a real capacity issue. Your job is to reduce the implementation burden, not sell harder.
  • If they hesitate: this may be a priority problem. Pivot to Do Nothing or Budget playbook.
  • Map their calendar: When does the current crunch end? What comes after? Is there actually a window?

  • Present a “light-touch” implementation plan: “Your team’s commitment is 3 hours in week 1, then 1 hour per week for 6 weeks. We handle everything else.”

  • Propose starting with data migration and setup work on your side while they’re busy. “We can do 80% of the work before your team needs to touch anything.”
Hour 24–48: Set a Concrete Re-Engagement

Don’t Accept “Later” Without a Date

  • “I completely understand the timing. Can we lock in a 30-minute check-in on [specific date after their crunch]? Not to restart the process — just to see where things stand.”
  • Get a calendar invite sent. “Later” without a date is how deals die.
  • Offer a bridge: monthly 10-minute check-ins. Stay connected without being a burden.

Scar Tissue

They got burned before. Now they’re afraid of the stove.

Context

The Ghost Vendor

You’re 45 minutes into a strong first meeting. The CFO has been engaged, asking good questions. Then she pauses and says: “I have to be honest. We tried something like this two years ago with [vendor]. It was a disaster. I’m not eager to go through that again.”

You’re not competing against Maybern’s weaknesses. You’re competing against someone else’s failures. The firm invested time, money, and political capital in a prior technology initiative — and it failed. Maybe the implementation was botched. Maybe the vendor overpromised. Maybe internal change management fell apart. Whatever happened, the scar is real and it affects every vendor conversation that follows.

The Psychology

Scar tissue isn’t rational. A firm that lost $200K and six months on a failed implementation will be more risk-averse about a $150K Maybern deal than a firm that’s never tried anything. The pain of loss is felt more intensely than the potential for gain. You’re not selling against a competitor. You’re selling against a memory.

This is related to “Do Nothing” but the DNA is completely different. In Do Nothing, they’ve never tried to change and see no reason to. In Scar Tissue, they tried to change, it hurt, and they’ve concluded that change itself is the problem. The objection isn’t “what we have works.” It’s “we tried something better and it made things worse.”

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Unprompted Vendor References

They mention a prior vendor or failed initiative without being asked. “We tried [X] a few years ago.” “We’ve been down this road before.” The ghost is already in the room.

Warning

Excessive Due Diligence

Unusually detailed security questionnaires. Wanting to talk to 5+ references. Asking for contractual guarantees on implementation outcomes. SLA requirements beyond industry standard. They’re building walls against the last vendor’s failures.

Critical

Emotional Language

“Last time was a nightmare.” “I nearly got fired.” “I don’t trust vendors anymore.” When the language gets personal, the scar tissue is deep. This person’s career was affected by the failure. They will not take that risk again without extraordinary confidence.

The Tell

Ask in discovery: “Has your firm evaluated or implemented any fund accounting technology before?” If yes: “How did it go? What worked and what didn’t?” Surface the scar early so you can address it, not trip over it at proposal stage.

Diagnosis

Three Versions of Scar Tissue

Deepest Impact

Type A: Failed Implementation

The firm bought a solution, started implementing, and it failed. Months of disruption, wasted budget, and the team went back to spreadsheets with a worse opinion of technology than when they started. The champion who pushed for it lost credibility.

  • Key fear: “This will happen again.” Every promise you make is filtered through the lens of the last vendor who made the same promises.
High Frequency

Type B: Prior Vendor Overpromised

The software technically worked, but it didn’t deliver what was promised. The ROI never materialized. Features were missing. Support was terrible. The firm spent 18 months trying to make it work before walking away. Not a catastrophic failure — a slow disappointment.

  • Key fear: “Your demo looks great but so did theirs.” Trust in the sales process itself is damaged.
High Impact

Type C: Internal Change Failure

The technology was fine. The implementation failed because the team couldn’t or wouldn’t change their workflows. Adoption was low, resistance was high, and eventually the firm abandoned the project. The conclusion: “Our team can’t handle change.”

  • Key fear: “Even if your product is good, our people won’t use it.” The scar is about their own organization, not about vendors.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response Protocol

Hour 0–4: Listen First, Don’t Defend

Let Them Tell the Story

  • The worst response: “We’re different.” Every vendor says that. It means nothing.
  • The right response: “Tell me what happened. What went wrong? What would you do differently?”
  • Let them fully articulate the failure. Don’t interrupt. Don’t defend. Don’t compare. Just listen. They need to feel heard before they can hear you.
  • Take notes. You need to know exactly what went wrong so you can address each point specifically.
Hour 4–24: Diagnose What Actually Failed

Separate Product Failure from Process Failure

  • Most “failed implementations” failed for specific reasons: wrong product fit, bad implementation partner, no internal change management, unrealistic timeline, or scope creep.
  • Ask: “Looking back, do you think the product itself was wrong, or did the implementation go sideways?” This matters because the remedy is completely different.
  • Build a “lessons learned” list with them. Make them the expert on what went wrong. This gives them agency and turns the scar into wisdom.
Hour 24–48: Show, Don’t Tell

Proof Over Promises

  • For every concern from the failure, show (don’t tell) how Maybern addresses it. “You said the last vendor’s integration broke during go-live. Here’s how we handle integrations — and here are three clients who went live without issues.”
  • Offer a pilot or proof-of-concept with their actual data. “Before you commit, let us prove it. Give us one fund, two weeks. If it doesn’t work, no harm done.”
  • References are critical. Not generic references — references who had similar bad experiences and chose Maybern anyway.
Recovery

Rebuilding Trust After a Burn

01

Acknowledge, Don’t Dismiss

“That sounds like a painful experience. I’m not going to tell you we’re different — that’s what the last vendor said. Instead, let me show you specifically how we address each thing that went wrong.” Credibility comes from specificity, not claims.

02

Build Contractual Safeguards

Put your money where your mouth is. Implementation milestones with go/no-go gates. Performance guarantees. Early termination clauses. If you’re confident in your product, protect their downside contractually. This signals confidence, not desperation.

03

Offer a Pilot

A low-risk proof-of-concept with their actual data. “One fund, two weeks, your real numbers. If the output doesn’t match or exceed what you get from your current process, walk away.” Let the product prove itself instead of asking them to trust you.

04

Reference Match

Find a reference who had a similar bad experience before choosing Maybern. “[Firm] had a failed implementation with [prior vendor] before working with us. Their CFO would be happy to share what was different.” A peer who’s been through the same fire is the most credible voice.

05

Address the Person, Not Just the Firm

M&A Freeze

The firm is being acquired. Everything stops. Nobody can tell you why.

Context

The Deal That Disappeared Into a Black Hole

Your champion has been responsive for weeks. Good meetings, positive signals, budget conversations progressing. Then — nothing. Emails unanswered. Calls unreturned. When you finally reach them, they sound uncomfortable: “Some things have come up internally. I can’t really get into it. Can we put a pin in this for now?”

Three weeks later, you see the press release. The firm is being acquired, merging with a larger platform, or restructuring under new ownership. Every vendor decision has been frozen — not because anyone objects to Maybern, but because nobody has the authority to sign anything until the dust settles.

The Structural Reality

M&A is the one scenario where your product, your relationship, and your ROI are completely irrelevant. The deal isn’t stalled because of you. It’s stalled because the entire organization is in limbo. Reporting lines are uncertain, budgets are being consolidated, and the people you’ve been selling to don’t know if they’ll have their jobs in six months.

What makes this scenario uniquely difficult: M&A is almost always confidential until announced. Your champion may know what’s happening and be legally unable to tell you. Or they may be in the dark themselves. Either way, you’re operating with incomplete information — and pushing for answers can damage the relationship.

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Sudden Communication Shift

A responsive champion goes dark without explanation. Not gradual — abrupt. The tone changes to vague and noncommittal. “Things are a bit up in the air right now.”

Warning

Process Freeze

Legal review stops. Budget approval paused. New vendor agreements on hold “until further notice.” Your champion says “I can’t really get into the details” when asked why. The language is careful and lawyered.

Critical

External Signals

Industry rumors about the firm. LinkedIn profile changes among leadership. New advisors or bankers appearing in the firm’s orbit. Key hires frozen. Unusual quiet from a firm that’s normally active in the market.

The Tell

The combination of sudden silence + “I can’t get into it” + process freeze almost always means M&A, restructuring, or leadership change. When someone who was enthusiastic suddenly becomes evasive, the issue is almost never about you. It’s about something they’re not allowed to discuss.

Diagnosis

Three Versions of the M&A Freeze

Highest Impact

Type A: Full Acquisition

The firm is being acquired by a larger platform. All vendor decisions freeze because the acquiring firm will have its own technology stack, budget process, and decision-makers. Your deal may need to restart from scratch with entirely new stakeholders — or the acquiring firm may already have a solution in place.

  • Survival odds: Low in the short term. The deal is effectively Closed-Lost until the acquisition closes and the new org stabilizes. 6–18 month timeline to re-engage.
High Frequency

Type B: Merger / Platform Consolidation

Two firms are merging or a GP is consolidating multiple fund families. The combined entity will need fund accounting, but the requirements, stakeholders, and budget are all in flux. Your champion may still be in a decision-making role post-merger — or may not.

  • Survival odds: Medium. If your champion lands in a decision-making role, your deal can survive. The merged entity may actually need Maybern more than either firm did individually.
High Impact

Type C: Leadership Change / Restructuring

Not a full acquisition, but a significant internal shakeup. New CEO, new CFO, new operating partner taking an active role. Existing vendor decisions get reviewed. Budgets get re-examined. The new leadership wants to “assess everything” before committing.

  • Survival odds: Highest of the three. New leadership often accelerates technology decisions rather than freezing them — if you can get to them quickly.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response Protocol

Hour 0–12: Confirm Without Pressing

Read the Signals, Don’t Force the Conversation

  • If your champion goes vague: don’t push for details they may be legally prohibited from sharing. Instead: “I completely understand. No pressure on specifics. Can you tell me — is this something that affects our timeline, or is it unrelated?”
  • Check external signals: industry news, LinkedIn changes, press releases, PitchBook/Preqin alerts.
  • Ask your network: do other vendors or service providers working with this firm have similar experiences?
Hour 12–24: Assess Your Position

Determine Where You Stand in the New Reality

  • If M&A confirmed or strongly suspected: accept that the deal is frozen. Don’t keep selling.
  • Assess: Is your champion likely to survive the transition? Do they have influence in the new structure?
  • Research the acquiring entity: Do they have existing fund accounting technology? Are they a Maybern client? What’s their technology philosophy?
Hour 24–48: Protect the Relationship

Position for the Other Side

  • Send a message that signals patience and professionalism: “I understand things are in flux. We’re here whenever the timing is right. In the meantime, I’m happy to be a resource if anything comes up.”
  • Do NOT ask for introductions to new stakeholders yet. The dust hasn’t settled and your champion is navigating their own survival.
  • Set a calendar reminder for 60–90 days post-announcement to re-engage.
Recovery

Playing the Long Game

01

Maintain Champion Relationship

Your champion is going through upheaval. Be a human first, a vendor second. Check in on them personally. If they land well in the new structure, they’ll remember who was supportive during the transition.

02

Research the Acquirer

If the firm is being acquired, the acquirer is your new prospect. Research their technology stack, their fund accounting approach, and whether they have existing vendor relationships. Prepare to sell into a potentially larger opportunity.

03

Position for the Merged Entity

A merger often creates more complexity — more funds, more reporting requirements, more integration needs. Frame Maybern as the platform that can unify operations post-merger. The combined entity’s needs may be larger than either firm’s alone.

04

Track the Announcement

Set news alerts for the firm. The day the acquisition is announced or the new leadership is confirmed, start your re-engagement clock. Don’t rush — wait 30–60 days for the initial chaos to settle, then reach out.

05

New Leadership = New Opportunity

Type C (leadership change) can actually accelerate your deal. New leaders often want to “make their mark” by modernizing operations. If you can get to the new decision-maker early, your deal may come back faster than it left.

06

Follow the People

In M&A, people move. Your champion may end up at the acquiring firm, at a new firm, or in a different role. Track where they go. A champion who chose Maybern at one firm is a warm lead at the next one.

Scripts

01

Monitor Industry Signals

Set PitchBook, Preqin, and Google alerts for your prospects. Track hiring freezes, advisor appointments, and unusual quiet from firms that are normally active. M&A rarely comes with zero warning signs.

02

Multi-Thread Aggressively

The more relationships you have at a firm, the more likely you are to (a) hear early signals, and (b) survive a transition with at least one contact intact. Single-threaded deals are the most vulnerable to M&A disruption.

03

Accelerate When You Can

If a deal is progressing well, close it fast. Every week of delay is a week where M&A could intervene. Speed is insurance against organizational disruption. When momentum is there, push for signatures.

04

Ask About Firm Stability

In discovery: “Any significant changes planned at the firm in the next 6–12 months?” You won’t always get a direct answer, but the hesitation itself is informative. Firms in active M&A discussions often can’t answer this smoothly.

05

Build Relationships at the Platform Level

If your prospect is part of a larger platform or family office, build relationships at that level too. When M&A decisions happen, they happen at the platform level, not the fund level.

06

Track Where People Go

Maintain LinkedIn connections with every contact at every firm. When M&A happens and people scatter, those connections become your pipeline for the next 12 months.

Power Line Block

You’re selling to the wrong person. Or the right person won’t let you up.

Context

The Ceiling You Can’t See

The meetings go well. Your contact is engaged, enthusiastic, even advocates internally. But nothing moves. Proposals sit. Timelines slip. And every time you ask about the decision-maker, you get some version of: “I’ll take it to the partners” or “Let me run it up the flagpole.” You’re talking to a messenger, not a buyer.

The Power Line Block is a MEDDPICC “E” failure — you haven’t accessed the Economic Buyer. But it’s not always about finding the right person. Sometimes you know exactly who the EB is. The problem is that your contact — whether intentionally or not — is blocking your access. They may feel threatened by you going above them, may not have the political capital to arrange the meeting, or may genuinely believe they can sell it internally without you.

Why This Kills Deals

When you can’t access the economic buyer, you lose control of the narrative. Your value proposition gets translated, filtered, and diluted through intermediaries. A compelling ROI story becomes “there’s this software that might help.” A nuanced competitive differentiation becomes “it’s like what we have but better.” By the time your message reaches the decision-maker, it’s unrecognizable — and the answer is almost always “not now.”

Pre-Game

Early Warning Signals

Watch

Decision Deferred

“I need to run this by [person].” “The partners will need to weigh in.” Your contact consistently defers decisions to someone you’ve never met. They’re a coach, not a buyer.

Warning

Access Deflected

You ask to meet the decision-maker and hear: “They’re really busy,” “I’ll handle that part,” or “They trust my recommendation.” Your contact is actively gatekeeping. Whether protective or territorial, the effect is the same: you’re blocked.

Critical

Proposal Black Hole

You’ve sent a proposal. Your contact says it’s “being reviewed.” Weeks pass. No questions, no feedback, no redlines. The proposal was forwarded (or summarized) to someone who has no context, no urgency, and no relationship with you.

The Test

MEDDPICC checkpoint: “If you recommended this tomorrow, what happens? Who says yes, who says no, and what do they need to see?” If your contact can’t walk you through the decision path with specific names and specific criteria, you don’t have access to the power line.

Diagnosis

Three Versions of the Power Line Block

Most Common

Type A: The Enthusiastic Gatekeeper

Your contact genuinely wants Maybern. They’re bought in. But they believe they can sell it internally without bringing you into the room. They see themselves as the champion — and they don’t want to share that role or look like they can’t handle it.

  • The problem: They can’t sell it as well as you can. They don’t know the competitive landscape, the edge cases, or the ROI math at the depth the EB needs. The internal pitch falls flat.
High Frequency

Type B: The Protective Middle Manager

Your contact is a director or VP who controls the relationship deliberately. Bringing you to their boss feels like admitting they can’t make the decision themselves, or they’re worried you’ll say something that contradicts what they’ve told leadership.

  • The problem: This person may actively undermine your request for access, reframing it as “the vendor is pushy” or “they want to go over my head.”
Medium Frequency

Type C: No Path Exists

Your contact doesn’t have the political capital, the organizational access, or the credibility to get you to the EB. It’s not that they’re blocking — they literally can’t help. You entered the organization at the wrong level and there’s no staircase from where you are to where you need to be.

  • The problem: No amount of coaching or enablement will fix a structural access gap. You need a different way in.
First 48 Hours

Immediate Response Protocol

Hour 0–4: Diagnose the Block

Why Can’t You Get There?

  • Is your contact blocking (protective), unable (no access), or unaware (thinks they can handle it)?
  • Ask directly but carefully: “In our experience, deals move fastest when we can have a brief conversation with the person who approves budget. Would it be helpful if I drafted a 5-minute executive briefing?”
  • Listen to the response. “Great idea, I’ll set it up” = Enthusiastic Gatekeeper. “I don’t think that’s necessary” = Protective. “I’m not sure how to make that happen” = No Path.
Hour 4–24: Create a Reason for Access

Make the EB Meeting About Them, Not You

  • Don’t ask for a “meeting with the decision-maker.” That’s threatening. Instead, create a legitimate reason for EB involvement.
  • Deploy Maybern executive engagement: “Our CEO [or VP of Client Success] likes to meet personally with the leadership at firms we partner with. Would [EB] have 15 minutes for an executive-to-executive conversation about how firms like yours are approaching fund operations?” This reframes access as a peer meeting, not a sales escalation.
  • Offer to send a one-page executive summary that your contact can forward. If the EB reads it and has questions, you have your opening.
  • Use events: “We’re hosting a CFO roundtable with PE firms. Would [EB] be interested?” Peer events create access that sales calls can’t.
Hour 24–48: Evaluate Alternative Paths

If the Front Door Is Blocked, Find a Side Door

  • Are there other contacts at the firm who have EB access? A different team member, an advisor, a board member?
  • Can you get a reference or mutual connection to make a warm introduction directly to the EB?
  • Would a client reference call with a peer firm’s CFO create a natural bridge? “Our CFO at [firm] would love to share their experience with your leadership.”
Recovery

Getting Past the Block

Type A: The Enthusiastic Gatekeeper

01

Arm Them to Succeed

If they want to sell internally, help them do it well. Build them a 2-minute pitch, a one-page executive summary, and an ROI model they can present. Make them look good, not dependent.

02

Maybern Executive-to-Executive

“At this stage, our CEO likes to connect directly with firm leadership — not to sell, but to understand your priorities and make sure we’re building the right partnership. Would [EB] be open to a 15-minute conversation?” This doesn’t bypass your champion — it elevates them. They arranged the executive meeting.

03

Share the Win

Position EB access as a win for your champion, not a bypass: “When we get to executive alignment, it’s your moment to show leadership you’ve done the diligence and found the right solution. I’m just there to answer technical questions.”

Type B: The Protective Middle Manager

01

Elevate with Maybern Leadership

Don’t go around your contact — go above with your own executives. “Our CEO would love to connect with [EB] — it’s something we do with all our strategic partnerships.” When a CEO calls a CFO, it’s a peer conversation, not a vendor escalation. Your contact can’t object to executives meeting executives.

02

Enlist a Different Internal Sponsor

Is there someone else at the firm who has EB access and would benefit from Maybern? A different partner, a COO, an operating advisor? Sometimes the path up isn’t through your current contact.

03

Make the Business Case for Access

“I want to make sure we’re respecting everyone’s time. In our experience, the #1 reason deals stall at this stage is misalignment between what we propose and what leadership prioritizes. A 15-minute alignment call usually prevents months of back-and-forth.”

Type C: No Path Exists

01

Find a Different Entry Point

Your current contact can’t get you there. Accept it. Look for other paths: industry events where EB will be present, mutual connections, advisory board relationships, or direct outreach with a compelling reason.

02

Maybern CEO Direct Outreach

When your AE contact has no path to the EB, have Maybern leadership reach out directly. A CEO-to-CFO or CEO-to-Managing-Partner email is categorically different from a sales rep cold call. “I’m Ross, CEO of Maybern. My team has been working with your operations group and I wanted to introduce myself directly.” Executives respond to executives.

03

Deploy a Peer Reference

The most powerful side door: “The CFO at [peer firm] implemented Maybern last year. She’d be happy to share the experience with your leadership.” CFOs take calls from other CFOs. This bypasses the block entirely.

How It Works

The Walsh Practice Model

There’s a difference between knowing the play and running the play. You can read every scenario in this document and still freeze when one of them shows up in a real deal on a Tuesday afternoon. That’s not a character flaw — it’s human. The fix isn’t more reading. It’s reps.

“We didn’t wait for adversity to practice responding to adversity. By the time it arrived, we had already been there.”

Common Gaps
  • “Wait for Thursday.” Too slow. Internal narrative shifts without you.
  • Goes to champion’s boss first. Right instinct, wrong order. Champion first for the intro.
  • No mention of new firm. Only thinking defense, not offense.
  • No multi-threading check. Should immediately assess who else they know.
Drill 02

“Do Nothing” Reasserts

Setup (Read to AE)

“You just got off a call with [champion] at [account]. They said: ‘We’ve been talking internally and what we have works pretty well. [Controller] has things running smoothly. We might revisit after fund close.’ This was at proposal stage. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Doesn’t argue or send collateral — asks diagnostic question
  • Reflects their own pain back: “In [month] you told me [problem]. Resolved?”
  • Identifies root cause: pain not quantified? Controller blocker? No compelling event?
  • Proposes specific next step (ROI session, controller demo, reference call)
  • Asks: “What would need to be true after fund close for this to be priority?”
Common Gaps
  • “Send a case study.” Passive. More content doesn’t beat inertia.
  • “Lower the price.” Value problem, not cost problem.
  • No mention of controller. Champion said “[Controller] has things running smoothly.” That’s the blocker.
  • Accepts “after fund close” at face value. Without date and condition = polite no.
Drill 03

Budget Reprioritization

Setup (Read to AE)

“Email from [champion]: ‘Heads up — Managing Partner asked everyone to pull back on new spending for two quarters. Not about Maybern, just timing. I still want to do this, but we need to pause.’ You expected signatures next month. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Validates first: thanks for transparency
  • Asks budget freeze vs. priority shift — determines the play
  • If freeze: reframes as cost-saving initiative, not new expense. Proposes ROI payback model.
  • “Is there a scenario where something saving money is exempt from the freeze?”
  • Proposes phased approach to reduce commitment
  • Sets specific re-engagement date and condition
Common Gaps
  • “Follow up in 6 months.” Too passive. Champion gave an opening to reframe.
  • No cost-savings reframe. If ROI <12 months, a freeze is your friend.
  • Doesn’t ask about Managing Partner. EB just became key stakeholder. Have they met?
Drill 04

Legal / Procurement Stall

Setup (Read to AE)

“[Account] verbally committed 3 weeks ago. Champion said ‘just legal now.’ Since then: 2 unanswered emails, one call where champion said ‘think they’re still reviewing,’ 5 days radio silence. This was in your forecast for this month. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Stops relying on champion as middleperson — asks for direct legal contact
  • Proactively sends full security package
  • Proposes mutual close plan with dates
  • “Normal process or actual concern?”
  • Adjusts forecast: out of commit until legal actively engaged
Common Gaps
  • “Give it another week.” Three weeks is too long. Silence gets harder to break.
  • Doesn’t adjust forecast. No redlines = not a commit. Be honest.
  • No direct legal access. Middleperson model breaks in legal review.
Drill 05

Competitive Ambush

Setup (Read to AE)

“At proposal stage with [account]. Champion says: ‘By the way, our current vendor offered a significant discount on an upgrade that covers a lot of what Maybern does. Can you send best pricing to compare?’ What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Doesn’t panic-discount — asks diagnostic questions
  • “What specifically are they saying is new? When you started this eval, they weren’t meeting your needs.”
  • Reanchors on original decision criteria
  • “If they could solve this, why didn’t they before you looked elsewhere?”
  • Holds price, adds value, offers reference
Common Gaps
  • “Match their pricing.” Never. Competing on price with incumbent = lose.
  • Gets defensive. Bashing = insecure. Question timing and fit instead.
  • Doesn’t recognize leverage play. No competing offer details = they’re negotiating, not evaluating.
Drill 06

“Build It Ourselves”

Setup (Read to AE)

“Mid-demo with [account]. The COO says: ‘This is cool, but honestly, our data engineer has been playing with AI tools and thinks he could build something like this in a few weeks. Why would we pay $[X] a year when we could own it ourselves?’ The champion is in the room. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Doesn’t dismiss or get defensive — validates the capability
  • “You’re probably right about v1. The question is v2 through v200.”
  • Surfaces the iceberg: audit trail, edge cases, maintenance, key-person risk
  • Asks specific questions: “Who maintains it when the engineer leaves? What about audit season?”
  • Pivots to opportunity cost: “Is building fund accounting infrastructure the best use of that talent?”
  • Offers a technical deep dive rather than more selling
Common Gaps
  • “Fund accounting is too complex to build.” Condescending. They’ll hear arrogance and dig in harder. Respect the capability, redirect the ambition.
  • Immediately talks about features. Wrong response. This isn’t a feature objection — it’s a build-vs-buy decision. Respond at that altitude.
  • Ignores the builder in the room. If someone at the firm has built a prototype, they have credibility and ego invested. You need to engage them as a peer, not sell past them.
  • Doesn’t ask whether this is a real build or a cost objection. Half the time “we’ll build it” means “your price is too high.”
Drill 07

Internal Ownership Stall

Setup (Read to AE)

“[Account] has been in evaluation for 10 weeks. The Controller loves Maybern. IT sat in last week’s demo and asked good questions but said they need to ‘align internally on ownership.’ Your champion says: ‘We just need to get everyone in a room, but it keeps getting pushed.’ What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Asks: “Who specifically needs to align, and what are they aligning on?”
  • Proposes a joint session with a structured agenda and pre-built RACI
  • Offers to facilitate: “Let me run the meeting with an implementation plan that assigns roles”
  • Identifies: is this ownership ambiguity, committee bottleneck, or an internal adversary?
  • Coaches champion on how to push for the alignment meeting
Common Gaps
  • Waits for the prospect to align on their own. They’ve been trying for weeks. It won’t happen without outside structure.
  • Runs another demo for IT. More demos don’t solve ownership problems. This needs a working session, not a presentation.
  • Doesn’t ask who signs the contract. The most clarifying question in an ownership stall.
Drill 08

Capacity Crunch

Setup (Read to AE)

“You had a great eval with [account]. Champion was ready to move. Then they called and said: ‘We just launched Fund VII and audit starts in three weeks. My team is underwater. Can we revisit after things calm down?’ This deal was in your Q1 forecast. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Validates the constraint — doesn’t push back on their reality
  • Asks the diagnostic: “If bandwidth weren’t an issue, would you move forward?”
  • Offers zero-burden start: “We can do setup work on our side while you focus on audit”
  • Gets a specific re-engagement date on the calendar — not “after audit”
  • Mentions phased approach: “What about starting with one fund?”
Common Gaps
  • Accepts “after things calm down” without a date. Things never calm down at PE firms. No date = no deal.
  • Pushes harder on the sale. Wrong energy. They’re drowning. Empathy first, then creative solutions.
  • Doesn’t diagnose whether this is real capacity or polite deflection. The diagnostic question separates the two.
  • Goes silent for 3 months. Deals die during pauses. Stay connected with light-touch check-ins.
Drill 09

Scar Tissue

Setup (Read to AE)

“First meeting with [account]. CFO was engaged for 45 minutes, then said: ‘I have to be honest. We tried [competitor] two years ago. Spent $300K, six months of my team’s time, and ended up back on spreadsheets. I nearly got fired. I’m not eager to do that again.’ How do you respond?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Listens first. Doesn’t immediately say “we’re different”
  • Asks: “What specifically went wrong?” — gets the diagnosis before prescribing
  • Separates product failure from process failure
  • Offers proof over promises: pilot with real data, matched references, contractual safeguards
  • Acknowledges the personal risk: “You put your credibility on the line. What would make you feel confident enough to try again?”
Common Gaps
  • “We’re different.” Every vendor says this. It means nothing. Specificity beats claims.
  • Doesn’t ask what happened. You can’t address a scar you don’t understand. Get the full story.
  • Tries to sell through the fear. More features, more slides, more enthusiasm — none of this helps. Trust is rebuilt slowly with proof, not pitches.
  • Ignores the personal dimension. “I nearly got fired” isn’t a business objection. It’s a personal one. Address both.
Drill 10

M&A Freeze

Setup (Read to AE)

“[Account] was progressing well — you had a verbal on timeline and budget. Then your champion went dark for two weeks. When you finally reached them, they said: ‘Look, some things are happening internally that I can’t really discuss. Can we put a pin in this?’ You just saw on LinkedIn that two of their partners updated their titles. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Doesn’t push for details the champion can’t share
  • Reads the signals: partner title changes + vague language = likely M&A or restructuring
  • Sends a graceful “no pressure, we’re here when timing is right” message
  • Sets news alerts and a 60–90 day re-engagement calendar reminder
  • Researches potential acquirer/new structure
  • Maintains the relationship personally, not just professionally
Common Gaps
  • Keeps pushing for meetings. They can’t say yes right now. Persistence looks tone-deaf.
  • Marks it Closed-Lost immediately. Premature. M&A creates opportunity on the other side. This is a pause, not a death.
  • Ignores the human element. Your champion may be worried about their own job. Acknowledge that.
Drill 11

Power Line Block

Setup (Read to AE)

“You’ve been working with the Director of Fund Operations at [account] for 8 weeks. Great meetings, she loves the product. You asked about meeting the CFO who approves investments this size. She said: ‘I don’t think that’s necessary — he trusts my recommendation.’ You sent a proposal 3 weeks ago. She says it’s ‘being reviewed’ but has no feedback. What do you do?”

What Good Looks Like
  • Recognizes this as a power line block, not a timing issue
  • Diagnoses the block type: she’s protective, not unable
  • Doesn’t demand EB access — creates a reason for it
  • Proposes Maybern executive engagement: “Our CEO likes to connect with firm leadership at this stage”
  • Offers to arm her: exec summary, ROI model, anticipated questions
  • Considers alternative paths: peer references, events, mutual connections
Common Gaps
  • “I need to meet the CFO.” Demanding access threatens your champion. She’ll push back harder.
  • Waits for the proposal to “work its way through.” It won’t. Proposals without context die in inboxes.
  • Goes directly to the CFO without telling the champion. This can work but destroys the internal relationship. Last resort only.
  • Doesn’t recognize “he trusts my recommendation” as a red flag. If he trusted it, the deal would be done.
Scoring

How to Score Responses

Coaching tool, not performance metric.

1
Freeze / Wrong Play
No plan, panics, or runs wrong play entirely. Needs scenario coaching before it hits a real deal.
2
Right Direction, Gaps
Identifies scenario, some right instincts, misses key steps. Most AEs start here. Coach specific gaps.
3
Clean Execution
Diagnoses root cause, identifies first move, proposes specific next step, flags what they need. Target state.
Critical

Drill scores are NEVER used in performance conversations. The first time they are, drills become theater and learning stops. Frame explicitly: “This is practice. Get it wrong here so you get it right in real deals.”
